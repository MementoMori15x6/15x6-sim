Memento & Mori | December 2025 – ongoing

The Board is not an ideology, nor a political doctrine. It is a map.

Every complex cooperative system—states, corporations, ecosystems, digital networks—occupies a position within a shared phase space defined by two interacting forces: productive metabolism and governance density. These forces determine how energy flows through the system, how cooperation is sustained, and how exploitation is constrained.

Within this landscape, systems do not remain static. They drift.  
Wars, technological shocks, corruption, institutional reforms, demographic changes, and cultural shifts all move a system across the map. Some movements are gradual, others abrupt. Over time, these trajectories carry systems toward regions of stability or toward attractors of decline.

The Board reveals that this landscape contains recognizable basins of attraction. Certain regions repeatedly generate long-lived cooperative systems. Others consistently produce stagnation, fragmentation, or collapse.

Among these basins, a narrow region appears again and again across history: a band in which cooperative systems demonstrate their highest survivability.  
This region is the **Stable Corridor**.

Within this corridor, the energetic balance of cooperation remains favorable. Productive output generates sufficient surplus to sustain institutions, while enforcement mechanisms remain efficient enough to suppress parasitic behavior without consuming excessive resources. Competition exists, but it remains bounded by shared rules that maintain trust and coordination.

Systems that inhabit this corridor do not eliminate conflict or exploitation. Rather, they maintain the capacity to manage these pressures without allowing them to dominate the system’s energy budget.

Over long historical timescales, the most durable societies appear to oscillate within this narrow band, adjusting institutions and incentives to remain within its boundaries.

### The Golden Path of Replicator Stability

A stable cooperative system must maintain a simple energetic inequality: the productive surplus generated by cooperation must exceed the energetic cost required to suppress exploitation. When this balance holds, institutions can maintain order without suffocating innovation or productive competition. When the balance narrows, systems drift toward instability, as policing costs rise and trust erodes. The most durable societies therefore occupy a narrow corridor in phase space where enforcement remains affordable and cooperation remains profitable. This corridor is not a moral ideal; it is the thermodynamic precondition for the long-term persistence of complex cooperation.

Outside this corridor, systems begin to drift toward less stable attractors.

In one direction lies the Rigidity Wall. Governance density grows increasingly heavy as institutions attempt to control economic and social behavior. Compliance burdens rise, innovation slows, and the productive metabolism of the system begins to weaken. As surplus declines, the cost of maintaining governance structures consumes a growing share of available energy.

In the opposite direction lies the Exploitationism Sinkhole. Enforcement mechanisms weaken or become corrupted, allowing parasitic actors to proliferate. Trust erodes, cooperative norms break down, and increasing resources must be devoted to detecting and suppressing cheating. As exploitation spreads, the system’s internal policing burden grows dramatically.

Although these two trajectories appear opposite in character, they converge toward the same thermodynamic outcome.

In both cases, the cost of maintaining cooperation rises while the surplus generated by cooperation falls.

At first this shift can be subtle. Institutions expand, regulations accumulate, compliance systems multiply, or enforcement mechanisms intensify. The system may appear stable on the surface, yet internally an increasing portion of its energy budget is redirected from productive activity toward monitoring, enforcement, and conflict resolution.

Gradually, the system begins to consume its own cooperative surplus.

This process continues until the fundamental energetic balance of cooperation is reversed.

At this boundary lies the universal fracture.

**Rule 13**  
“When the cost of suppressing cheaters exceeds the cooperative surplus, the system fractures.”

Once this threshold is crossed, cooperation becomes energetically unsustainable. Institutions designed to preserve order instead accelerate fragmentation. Enforcement expands, yet compliance falls. Productive actors withdraw, opportunistic actors multiply, and the system’s internal friction rises faster than its capacity to generate surplus.

The attractor basin changes.

What once functioned as a cooperative system begins to dissolve into competing factions, parasitic structures, or rigid hierarchies incapable of adaptation. Collapse may occur rapidly or unfold over generations, but the underlying mechanism remains the same: the energetic foundation of cooperation has failed.

This is not a moral judgment. It is a structural constraint.

Complex systems can only sustain cooperation when the energetic cost of maintaining trust remains lower than the surplus that trust produces.

The Board does not determine which societies will succeed or fail. It does not prescribe ideologies or political doctrines.

It reveals the terrain through which cooperative systems must navigate.

Between the rigidity wall and the exploitation sinkhole lies a narrow region where cooperation remains energetically possible. Systems that remain within this corridor can endure for centuries. Systems that drift too far from it eventually encounter the same boundary.
