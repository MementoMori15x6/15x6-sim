Politics has never been exempt from the laws that govern the universe. We have only arrogantly thought otherwise.

Every civilisation that ever rose and fell might have been seen as a far-from-equilibrium replicating system, perhaps even following patterns similar to those in hydrothermal vents, cancer cells, ant colonies, corporations, and nations.[1](about:blank#fn1)

Until 2025, we lacked two major variables:

- Exhaustive, real-time data on every fiscal, demographic, and capital flow inside a polity.
- The computing power to turn that data into a live thermodynamic ledger.

Both constraints disappeared in the last decade.

What remains could be considered building blocks of a potential scientific theory. A microscope shining a light on the health of replicating systems, revealing patterns we can diagnose, discuss, and test together.

This manuscript places several recorded polities — from Rome to Seoul, from Mao to Washington — under that microscope and watches what might be the same physics play out, with a fidelity we can refine through open discourse.

The results are somewhat uncomfortably simple, but they also invoke intrigue, and invite validation:

- Measurements suggest every known collapse can be traced to violations of similar patterns, predictable decades in advance — a claim ready for empirical testing.
- Data indicates the contemporary West is showing dynamics akin to previous hegemons, open to scrutiny via the entropy ledger.
- The main difference now is that we can observe system health in near real time, shining a light on replicating dynamics, so we may score proposed treatments before implementation, and before potential ruin.

There is no ideology in these pages. There is no left, no right, no hope, and no despair. There is only measurement — and an invitation for experts and interested communities to test, refine, and expand our niche 15×6 simulation script.

The fifteen rules are not moral commandments. They are proposed physical constraints, discovered independently by biology, thermodynamics, and computer science, open to your critique and collaboration.
