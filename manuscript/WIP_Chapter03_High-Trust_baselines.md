Chapter 3: High-Trust Baselines – Biological Eusociality as Thermodynamic Foil
Replicating systems that achieve long-duration far-from-equilibrium stability must export entropy efficiently while suppressing cheaters at minimal cost. The Rome arc in Chapter 2 illustrated a human polity where mutualism eroded, suppression costs escalated, and Rule-13 parasitism accumulated toward cascade. To understand the opposite end of the spectrum — systems with near-ideal thermodynamic health — we begin with a biological baseline where coordination emerges "for free" from kin selection and chemical enforcement.
The stable mature eusocial ant colony (e.g., Formicinae or Atta species) serves as our canonical high-trust foil. In these superorganisms, reproduction is monopolized by the queen, workers are sterile, and high relatedness (haplodiploidy, r ≈ 0.75 among sisters) aligns individual fitness with colony success. Resource flow occurs via trophallaxis and pheromone-mediated task allocation, with no markets, property, or personal profit. Cheater detection (Rule 13) is extraordinarily efficient: worker policing, oophagy, and chemical cues suppress reproductive parasitism to <5–10% in healthy phases.
